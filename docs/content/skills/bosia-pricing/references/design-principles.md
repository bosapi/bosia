# Pricing — principles

## Lineage

- open-design `skills/frontend-design` — comparison-layout discipline.
- open-design `skills/marketing-psychology` — anchor pricing, recommended-tier framing.

## Three-tier rationale

Three tiers is the cognitive sweet spot:

- One tier = "take it or leave it" (no anchor).
- Two = "cheap vs. expensive" (forces choice).
- Three = "low / recommended / loud" (the middle anchors).
- Four+ = decision paralysis.

If you have legitimate four product shapes, prefer two pricing pages (e.g. "Teams" vs. "Enterprise") over four tiers on one page.

## The recommended tier

Make it the middle. Mark it visually (border, badge, slight elevation) — not louder by 3×. The point is to guide, not shout.

## Comparison table vs. tier cards

Tier cards sell the value prop. The comparison table answers "but does it have X?". They're complements, not duplicates — don't repeat the same 4 features in both.

## Monthly default

People price-anchor on the monthly number even when buying annual. Show monthly first; reveal the annual savings via a toggle, not by burying the monthly.

## FAQ purpose

Pre-empt the "I'd buy but…" objections: refunds, contract length, plan changes, seat math, taxes, security.
